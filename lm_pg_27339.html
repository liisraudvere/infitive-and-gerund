Gerunds and infinitives

What are gerunds and infinitives? 

Gerund is the -ing form of the verb (E.g. shooting, swimming, drinking)
Infinitive is the base form of the verb (E.g. to shoot, to swim, to drink; note that sometimes the 'to' can be left out)

Gerunds and infinitives are forms of verbs that are used like nouns.
We need to use these 'nouns' made out of verbs in places where we cannot use verbs.

Verbs can only be in place of verbs. If we want to use a verb in any other place (as subjects or objects), we need a gerund or infinitive form.

We cannot use a verb in place of a subject: We CANNOT say 'Swim is good for your health' or "Shoot is an important skill for soldiers.". 
We cannot use a verb in place of an object. We CANNOT say 'I want go' or 'I like swim'. 

Instead we need to use 
  • the gerund (a noun, made from the -ing form of the verb) (tegevus, nt. joonistamine, luuletamine) OR
  • the infinitive (to form of the verb) (-da/-ta tegevusnimi, nt. joosta, luuletada)
So the correct versions are: 'Swimming is good for you' and 'I want to go'.

How do I know if I need to use the gerund or the infinitive?

There are very few general rules about when we use the gerund and when the infinitive.

Some verbs are followed by the gerund and some by the infinitive and these combinations just need to be learned.
Sometimes both options are possible without any change in meaning.
With other verbs the meaning can change considerably depending on whether you use the gerund or the infinitive.

Although we have similar rules in Estonian, most combinations cannot be directly translated and we cannot often use Estonian rules.

In general gerunds are used
  • after certain verbs (E.g. I enjoy drinking a cup of coffee in the morning)
  • as subjects (E.g. Cycling is one of the most popular sports.)
  • after prepositions (E.g. I am afraid of going home alone.)
  • after some special phrases (E.g. I we had some problems finding the answer)

Infinitives are used 
  • after certain verbs (E.g. I want to buy a new car.)
  • after question words (how, what, where, whether) (E.g. He asked me where to go and what to take.)
  • after adjectives (E.g. It was impossible to give him what he wanted.)
  • as subjects (when followed by the verbs be, seem, appear) (E.g. To go there was the right decision)
  • after certain nouns (the ones formed from the verbs that are usually followed by infinitives) (E.g. He made a promise to give me the money.)

There are also cases when we need to use infinitives without to, for example
  • after certain verbs (can, could, dare, do, help, let, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would)
  • after certain expressions (had better, would rather, would sooner, why not, why should I/you/… [not]) 
  • after certain verb + object combinations (make someone and let someone)
And finally there are verbs that can be followed by either the gerund or the infinitive.

Sometimes there is a change in meaning, sometimes they are used in different ways and sometimes they are interchangable.
Verbs followed by gerund or infinitive with no change in meaning

Verbs followed by gerund or infinitive with a change in meaning
forget / remember
INFINITIVE with regard to the future (E.g. Remember to switch off the lights.)
GERUND with regard to the past (E.g. Do you remember switching off the lights?)

go on
INFINITIVE start something new (E.g. Go on to read.)
GERUND continue with the same action (E.g. Go on reading.)

regret
INFINITIVE with regard to the future (E.g. I regret to say that.)
GERUND with regard to the past (E.g. I regret saying that.)

stop
INFINITIVE interrupt another action (E.g. I stopped to smoke.)
GERUND terminate (E.g. I stopped smoking.)

try
INFINITIVE do something complicated (E.g. Try to solve this riddle.)
GERUND do it and see what happens (E.g. Try talking to him.)
